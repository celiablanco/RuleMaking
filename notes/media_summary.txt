Life does not just adapt to its environment -- it reshapes it. But how could simple chemistry first acquire this capacity? Using a mathematical model that couples replicating molecules to the environment they modify, we show that a sharp threshold separates two regimes: one where chemical populations remain passive, and one where they actively stabilise the conditions for their own persistence. Crossing this threshold requires that environmental modifications persist long enough to feed back on population dynamics, creating a form of chemical memory. The results suggest that the transition from passively adapting chemistry to actively environment-shaping chemistry was rare and conditional, depending jointly on feedback strength, cost, and environmental history.
